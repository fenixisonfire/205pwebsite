User Acceptance Testing

Overview


Throughout the project, testing has been an ongoing process. Flaws in the site’s codebase could lead to an unresponsive site or server crashes, resulting in expensive disruptions. Furthermore, ensuring site usability and intuitiveness is fundamental. Failures in user friendliness signify a failure in the goal of the site being an easily accessible reference point for Peach frontend development.

User testing was critical to the project. We conducted user tests with varying degrees of formality during the project development cycle. These ranged from observing the ease of how users navigate the site, to structured interviews. To determine who we should include in the user tests, we first had to identify which demographic would likely make up the target user. This is outlined in the Common User section.

Of course client feedback was also vital. As Dr. Ramachandran is the founder of Peach, and as it is he who will decide the design philosophies of the site, his feedback to site mockups and iterations strongly shaped the development process. The client was also able to provide clear information as to who the end user will be, allowing us to further refine user tests.

Common User


As outlined by the client during the requirements gathering phase, the primary user of the site will be front end web developers commissioned to work on the Peach project. Accordingly, they are assumed to be competent in HTML and CSS. They are not necessarily familiar with the Peach infrastructure, or React.js however.

Questionnaire


During development, our team created a questionnaire to better understand the common user and how users interact with the platform, and whether users understand the need for the Design Guide system. The questionnaire primarily focused on how users responded to the site’s look and feel. The intuitiveness of navigation and overall information layout was our main concern.

  • Questionnaire 2

  • Questionnaire 3

From the 53 responses we gathered, ratings of overall site aesthetic was overwhelmingly positive, with 90.5% of respondents rating the sites design as either ⅘ or 5/5.

Questionnaire Results


  • There were similar results regarding the site navigation system.


  • A small number (13) of respondents had used design guides or specifications in previous projects. However, the tendency of developers to refer to these guides appears to vary markedly.


  • Accordingly, during interviews we sought to address why this was.


  • While most of the users made use of the live code editor feature and interactive D3, a sizeable minority did not. Accordingly, this would appear to show that the site should advertise these features more heavily towards users.


  • As expected, the questionnaire showed that developers that have used D3 remain distinctly in the minority (less than 20%). Among those unfamiliar with D3, the majority found the visualisations tab to be helpful, however nearly 20% were unsure of the page’s usefulness. We sought to clarify this during interviews.


  • For the final question, an overwhelming majority found the site to be user friendly, with 92.5% of respondents rating intuitivity as ⅘ or 5/5.


  • This questionnaire confirmed many of the successes of the site. However more could be done to advertise site features such as the live code editor. Furthermore, a sizeable minority of users were unsure of the usefulness of the D3 tab.

Interview


Our team conducted 5 semi structured interviews. Three of the interviews were conducted with developers who had worked on projects that required the use of design guides. We sought to understand the limitations of a design guide and why developers may choose to eschew referencing them. All interviewees stated that the biggest frustration of provided design systems, was the poor layout of information, particularly with regard to information overload, with an abundance of superfluous information. They further stated that vague or imprecise information was also a source of annoyance, with information. Both respondents said the drop down sections within our site did much to alleviate information overload. They found information concise, however they found particularly the components page to be overly long.

Two interviews were conducted with questionnaire respondents who answered that they weren’t sure of the usefulness of the D3 site. Both participants felt that the information on the tab was not thorough enough to constitute as a tutorial page for those unfamiliar for D3. They felt it was also not expansive enough to cover all Peach visualisation use cases.

The respondents positively reacted towards the information layout and ease of navigation. However, in future site iterations, especially as it grows, the components page should be further compartmentalized. In future iterations, the D3 page should either make its purpose as a showcase more clear, or be developed to include more basic information with regard to setting up D3 visualisations.

Interview Results


Overall, the results of the interviews and questionnaires show that the site design and usability was well received by the user demographic. However, there were issues that needed to be addressed. Site features, such as the live code editor, need to be more prominently advertised. Furthermore we also need to make the purpose of the D3 tab more clear.

Compatibility Testing

The Design Guide website proved to be compatible with the following browsers: Google Chrome on Mac, Safari on Mac, Safari on iPad, Safari on iPhone, Google Chrome on Windows and many others. This is as expected since the technologies we have used are all supported by modern web browser applications. Our testing bench was set up on safari and Mozilla Firefox. We tested many platforms and all passed flawlessly.
